We Have Courage to Speak Up

Lily Adong (24) lives in Northern Uganda, in a small village called Mutema. She is a volunteer Independent Resource Tracker, specially trained by ActionAid to help ensure that public funds for local development are being spent in the way that they should. Uganda is one of the most corrupt countries in the world and allocated public services and funds often never reach her remote village. She has a tough job but even though she has only been tracking funds since March 2013 she has already won her first victory.

In 2012, the community in Mutema had been promised funds from the Northern Uganda Social Action Fund (NUSAF), a programme set up by the Ugandan central government to support communities in Northern Uganda to develop sustainable initiatives.

A local NUSAF lead took up the role to distribute the fund; however, instead of initiating improvements in the village, he started demanding money from the community in exchange for their participation in the programme. This was completely illegal and corrupt!

Lily Adong comments, "these are poor people and this man was saying 'if you give me money, I will make sure goats, seeds and water will be given to your village,' and people were paying!! These people fight hard to find this money which was anything from 8,000-10,000 shillings (approximately 20 euro). But after money was given to this man the village saw none of the changes promised. It was an outrage."

With the support of ActionAid, a ‘Reflect’ group was set up in Mutema and from the initial meetings of the group, it was clear there were several other cases of corruption. It was discovered that in relation to the re-construction of the community Health Centre, a builder had been chosen but no construction had started. There were also promises of a fresh water system for the village which had never been acted on. Finally, reports also emerged of local health workers stealing medication from the health Centre and selling it illegally. "We sat down and discussed all these cases of local government corruption at the meeting and decided to make a petition," says Mutema’s ‘Reflect’group chairman, Okello Madakeo.

Lily Adong along with other ActionAid Voluntary Resource Trackers started to detail these cases. In addition, they also undertook a larger investigative study of the development plans for the area. For Lily this was a long and tiresome process as her village is about 40km from the main government offices in the District of Amuru and she had to go there many times by bicycle.

The ‘Reflect’ group delivered a four-page letter signed by 170 people in the community and Lily Adong and five others cycled to the Amuru district headquarters to deliver it. They also sought a meeting with one of the highest officials in the authority, the District Resident Commissioner (DRC). "We waited outside for two hours before we entered. We delivered the signatures and our letter and explained the cases of corruption by local government in our village, "said Lily Adong." During the meeting the District Resident Commissioner actually took out his phone and started to call around to investigate what we were saying to him. We couldn't believe it!"

Ultimately it was discovered that the person officially contracted and listed to complete the construction work at the Health Centre, was completely different to the contractor chosen by the district council. It was uncovered that the local board of directors of the Health Centre had received a bribe and had instead hired another company who had taken the money and not started the work. Lily Adong and the other IRTs cycled back to the village after their three hour meeting, tired but happy.

Since then, construction work has begun at the Health Centre, the water pump has been set up and the NUSAF programme lead has since been arrested. Motivated by this success, the group are now working on improving the road to Amuru which will make the journey to their nearest and largest market far easier and shorter.

"We were blind but now we have the confidence and courage to question where public money is being spent and my training as a Volunteer Resource Tracker helped me to do this," said Lily.

Lilly is one of the few to complete secondary school and has very good English. `"I have graduated from school which is not the case for many others girls in my village so I feel it is my duty to do something to fight this corruption. We need to stand up and fight - if not we will never see the situation change in our village."

Did you know?

Uganda is deemed to be one the most corrupt countries in the world. ActionAid worsk directly with country partners and local grassroots organisations helping directly those who need it the most.

Did you know?

More than 60 percent of people in Amuru live below the official poverty line. The average income for a household in the area is less than 2 euro a day.

Fact: Reflect Group

ActionAid uses the 'REFLECT' methodology all over the world. With ‘REFLECT’ the community comes together to discuss common issues that affect their lives and they are supported to take action to address these issues.

For example, in 2013, to address low levels of income in Amuru, ActionAid set up Village Savings Groups for female smallholder farmers. This initiative aimed to increase overall household income through the support of microcredit and savings schemes. Members can save money and borrow from their savings to meet key needs such as their funding their children’s education, paying medical expenses and investing in small business or seeds to increase crop yield. The best performing savings group in 2013 in Amuru was the “Pajaa Reflect Group” which now has 48 members. Together they saved an incredible Ugx11,913,600/= [€3,504] by the end of December 2013.

Fact: Independent Resource Trackers (IRTs)

IRTs are citizens who helps keep the authorities on their toes! ActionAid has trained 60 resource trackers in Amuru alone and has given them each a bicycle so they can travel the area to monitor schools, Health Centres and various government programs.

Everyday 16 Women in Uganda Die Giving Birth

Margaret (33) sits with her newborn daughter outside the Lacor Health Centre III in Amuru town. It’s 11am. Six hours ago she gave birth and an hour later , she was sent out of the hospital ward and asked to sit with the other patients under a big tree just outside the clinic. Some of these patients are pregnant and the rest have new-born babies in their arms. Every morning at 6am they are sent out of the clinic and are allowed back into the ward in the afternoon and that’s only if they have not need sent home in the meantime.

"Immediately after I gave birth they cut the umbilical cord and I was sent outside, I was so tired after the birth and the walk to get here ", says Margaret. She lives in the village of Toro Camboo, a few kilometres from the Health Centre in Amuru.

"When I went into labour, my husband borrowed a motorcycle for us, but it broke down on the road so we were left to walk the rest of the way. I was with my mother in law. When we reached the gate we saw the Health Centre was locked. We stood outside for half an hour before a guard came and opened. I was so afraid of giving birth in the middle of it all. I was in severe pain and the midwife was nowhere to be seen”.

“When I was eventually allowed in, it was actually my mother in law who delivered the baby. It was probably 5am when I gave birth but at 6am we were sent outside again. It was really cold, so I wrapped my daughter in some sheets to keep her warm," says Margaret.

Margaret has now seven children. She gave birth to her first almost 10 years ago early one morning, alone in the middle of the road and her other five children were delivered in her small hut at home assisted by her mother in law.

"You have to have everything with you when you go to the Health Centre to give birth - sheets, clothes, soap - so when I started to have contractions my mother in law went in search of these items while I started to walk on the road to the clinic. My contractions were so strong but I thought it was better that I just went ahead on my own in case I didn’t arrive to the clinic on time," says Margaret, who never gave a thought to ask her husband Simon to accompany her. In Uganda men never feature in the birth of their children and women are left to look after themselves.

"I remember giving birth to my first. I lay down at the side of the road and delivered her all by myself. When the baby came out, I grabbed a piece of hard grass, which was very sharp. I cut the umbilical cord with it and then I unravelled a piece of wire that I had in my bag and tied the umbilical cord. When my mother in law came along eventually, we went back home with the child. My daughter who is now ten years old is called Ayoo, or in our local language, Luo. Here children are named according to the circumstances by which they came into the world. Ayoo means "on the road".

For Margaret, there is no difference between giving birth in hospital or at home.

"This was my first time giving birth at the clinic but I prefer to give birth at home. The hospital is obviously important if problems arise but there is more care and love at home." The clinic is total under resourced so it is no wonder that Margaret thought the staff were rude and abrupt and found the whole experience to be traumatic.

The women sitting exhausted under a tree, 10 meters from a sick bed but waiting patiently until the ward has been cleaned

Did you know?

A Ugandan woman gives birth to an average of 7 children. The birth rate is about three percent - one of the worlds’ highest. Half the population is under the age of 15.

Did you know?

A quarter of all children in the district of Amuru are orphans. This means that many families have adopted one or more children often from their immediate family who have died of disease or as a result of the war.

Facts

Every day 16 women in Uganda die as a result of childbirth - one of the world's highest rates of deaths relating to pregnancy. They die mainly because of complications during birth because so many women give birth at home with only the support of a traditional midwife or relative. Many health centres and hospitals are not much better as there is a lack of doctors, nurses and equipment to provide adequate patient care.

These are the things that can really make an impact and change lives. Life is Good When We Have Enough Food

Concy Akwero (38) has spent her day harvesting rice. As she painstakingly thrashes each sheath of rice by hand you are brought back to centuries-old farming methods. It's backbreaking and time consuming but this is how women here in Northern Uganda farm.

"The harvest is not good this year," she says, "There is nothing, last year I harvested five sacks, this year I have no more than three small buckets." Compared to a harvest of 500kg last year, this year she has no more that 50 kg of rice to feed her family. The drought this year is causing devastation among the farming community in this otherwise fertile corner of Africa. It's the same story with Concy's bean crop – the yield is simply pitiful.

Besides rice and beans, Concy also grows sweet potatoes, peanuts and millet. The rainy season has just set in and small farmers are praying that the rain continues so their next harvest might be better before the dry season comes again. Concy's husband Patrick trades their surplus grain and otherproduce in the local market in the village. This year most of their harvest will be needed by the family and as rice is four times more expensive outside of harvest season they nearly always feel they are living hand- to-mouth.

Concy has 6 children from a 2yr old to 15yr old including an orphan to take care of. With pride Concy shows us around her kitchen inside the small where her two eldest children sleep. Clothes hang from the ceiling inside the hut. She hopes that she can support her children's education as she herself is illiterate and can't even write her own name.

"It's hard to survive mid-season from June to July. During these months we usually only eat once a day in the evening. When we have enough food, we eat twice a day," says Concy Akwero. It's starting to rain nicely now so we are hoping that this year's drought was a one-off.

"When I'm well rested, and there is enough food, life is good," she says.

Did you know?

Female smallhold farmers are very effective and are almost fully organic, but they often lack an understanding of trading and business. Last year ActionAid Ireland funded 22 Village Savings Groups which had an average female participation of 85%. The savings groups are aimed at increasing overall household income through the support of micro credit and savings schemes for female farmers like Concy. To strengthen engagement in this programme, in 2013 ActionAid also offered agriculture training using Savings and Credit Cooperatives principles (SACCOs) and 47 leaders from 22 savings groups benefitted from this training in the districts of Amuru and Nwoya in Northern Uganda.

Fact on Agriculture

Only about 10 percent of the land in Amuru is cultivated. During the bloody conflict and war against the LRA (Lord's Resistance Army) in Northern Uganda, most of the local population were displaced into refugee camps. During this time, land which had previously been cultivated fell into disrepair and upon returning to the land, the community were faced with a huge task to cut the overgrown bush. 98% of farmers use hand tools to cultivate and clear land. ActionAid supported farmers returning to their land with oxen to assist with the clearing. Groups shared the care of the animals and took turns to plough the fields in advance of planting.

It’s the little things like the provision of seeds, tools or livestock, the teaching of new farming methods or ensuring a supply of water that can really impact and shape their lives. You can provide these and help end a lifetime of hardship, total poverty and uncertainty.

A Bridge has Changed our Lives

“In all the time I can remember in the village, it was dangerous to cross the river”, says 62-year-old Alex Ojera from Oticihi Village in northern Uganda. “We were afraid that our children would be washed away during the rainy season, so sometimes we didn’t send them to school and pregnant women could not balance on the slippery logs to reach the healthcare”.

Previously to cross the river you would have had to walk 100 meters over slippery, muddy logs but now, as a result of the new bridge crossing even in the rainy seasons is no longer a problem.

“Things have really improved in the village since it was built”, says Obuti Bosco, who has been a driving force behind the building of the bridge.

The bridge is in place as a result of the community coming together to lobby local authorities and to demand improved infrastructures for their isolated village.

Alex Ojera explains the benefits that the bridge has brought to the whole community. “Now our children always go to school in the rainy season and we’re not afraid that they will be washed away - this bridge will save many lives; if our neighbours are sick they can go to the health centre by motorcycle; the traffic to and from our village has improved as well and this has also helped trade; for women, it has also been easier to go to market with their produce so they sell more and this means more money to buy food and to support our children’s education”.

Obuti Bosco who led the way in lobbying the authorities and helped to physically build the bridge in 2011 continues, “I am very pleased that we convinced the authorities to build this bridge”. While the Department of Engineering paid for the building materials and arranged for food and a small wage to be paid, the men in the village came together and worked on it and in the end we built it quite quickly. He added “you can’t believe how this has changed our lives”.

Education: Don’t Dare to Dream

15-year-old Mwaka Santo is pictured here in the village of Otichi where he lives in northern Uganda. He stands in his bare feet. "I want to study and I dream of becoming a pilot." It is unlikely he will get the chance to become one – but if you take away his chance of an education you take away his dreams. Right now he's not in school and it’s not looking like he will be able to return to school. Like many other children living in poverty in northern Uganda, he has to work. Santo works burning trees to make a type of charcoal that he sells to support his family and to help save for his education.

It’s hard, dirty work. Like the burning charcoal, his chances of returning to school is disappearing into the warm air of Amuru. Mwaka Santo already fears he will end up like one of 100 million young people in southern Africa who are sentenced to a lifetime of hard labour, having to live hand to mouth simply to get by.

Amuru has a very young population with those under 18 accounting for 55% of the total population. Usually this would depict a high dependency ratio on the adult population, however, Amurua the case is reversed. As a result of the armed conflict, many adults and parents were either killed or abducted leaving children to turn to relatives for support or fend for themselves in refugee camps.

In Uganda monthly school fees must be paid. As most families take in relatives, this extra burden of care means only the lucky ones get to go to school – unfortunately Santo is not one of them.

None of Santos siblings have been educated. He still looks to the sky and dreams of life as a pilot. We all have the right to an education and the right to dream of a better life. Why should Santos be different?

ActionAid Ireland and ActionAid Uganda are working to ensure that children like Santo will have access to quality education. We do this by strengthening the community to demand that governments and authorities are accountable to create an education system that is accessible to all.

Florence is Afraid for her Life

Florence Anzo is 26 years old and has four children with her husband. She’s afraid to go home and has nowhere to go.

“My children and I never had enough food. My husband used to take everything from us even the small amount of money I used to earn by selling cassava roots. If I would say anything against him, he would threaten me with a pangaen (a large knife). When this happened I usually fled into the bush and hid between shrubs and trees afraid for my life, hoping he wouldn’t find me. One particular time he got mad he took everything from the kitchen. The hut was completely empty and I didn’t know what to do or how to feed my children”, she says as she shows some of the scars she has on her arms after the knife attacks from her husband.

This morning, Florence Anzo has walked 25 kilometres with her baby on her back to look for help from the ActionAid supported Women Protection Centre in Amuru town.

“My children don’t go to school like the other children. I can’t afford it. I’m here desperately looking for help”, she says as she sits in the waiting room with her daughter.

Since Florence Anzo first met her husband, he has been beating and threatening her. They met in one of the refugee displacement camps that were established as a result of the armed conflict which tore northern Uganda apart until 2010. It’s not the first time that Florence has come to the centre in search of help. But this seems like the last straw. Her husband and his second wife (polygamy is common in northern Uganda) has thrown Florence Anzo and her four children out of their shared home. She is now homeless; she has no income or food and is worried about her children as some of them have become ill. Moreover she is afraid that her husband will find out where she is and suddenly reappear.

“I am so grateful to the Women’s Protection Center as they make sure that he knows he can’t just show up here. Either he must come here in an organised meeting or someone from the centre can relay information to him”, explains Florence Anzo breastfeeding little Aciro Wichily whose name means “Braved the Pain” in English. Generally children’s names are given based on the circumstance in which they were brought into the world.

“I was bleeding in the week leading up to the birth and my only outfit was covered with blood. I just couldn’t go to the hospital in those clothes”, she explains.

When the contractions finally came, there was no one to help Florence with transportation to the health centre so she had to give birth at home helped by her mother in law. She bled heavily for two weeks after birth until she finally got help to go to the health centre which is located next to the Women Protection Centre.

“They gave me some clean clothes, but my daughter is not well so the battle is not over yet” says Florence Anzo while she tries to wipe away her tears unnoticed. Florence’s turn has come, Director of the Centre Josephine Laker call her in to write up a new incident report and to organize the next actions for her case.

7 Things You Don’t Want to Know about Women in Amuru

  1. 57% of women in Amuru live below the poverty line
  2. Just 45% of women and girls over 10 years in Amuru can read
  3. No woman in Amuru owns land
  4. Widows are often driven from their land when their husband dies as they have no ownership rights
  5. 7 out of 10 homes experience domestic violence both psychological and physical
  6. Women in Amuru have an average of eight children to support including orphans as a result of the war
  7. 65% of girls and 45% of boys are forced to leave school in 7th grade

The Spread of Violence Against Women

The number of women and young girls who are raped or subjected to violence is very high in the district of Amuru in northern Uganda. There is no exact figure on the amount of women suffering. in the women's protection centre in Amuru, a week doesn’t go by without multiple cases of violence and rape. This centre is supported by ActionAid Ireland and ActionAid Uganda.

“We help women in several ways, we provide counselling, legal advice and personal advice. We also give basic skills in finance and business so that these women are not getting scammed when selling their vegetables at the market or on the side of the road”, says Josephine Laker, director of the Women’s Protection Centre.

As a Village Sponsor you support the Women’s Protection Centre by providing:

  1. A space for new cases of violence, rape and domestic violence to be recorded
  2. Legal advice and assistance for women how who are suffering domestic violence
  3. Shelter for the night, if there is no other accommodation
  4. Mediation services between husband and wife
  5. Follow up visits to the couple’s private home
  6. Educational support for women such as basic literacy and numeracy classes
  7. Lobbying of police and other authorities to demand the protection of women

Irene Oneka, legal advisor to the Women's Protection Center explains that the it is often the husband who is the direct cause of the women coming to the centre to seek help. He has either beaten or raped the woman, or he simply cannot support the family.

“Previously, women had no real hope. They did not dare complain and women like Florence suffered in silence but now as more and more people are hearing about the centre and our services and that we actually solve problems we are even seeing men come voluntarily to participate in mediation meetings- now that’s a first I thought I’d never see!”, says Irene Oneka

The suffering in the life of women and girls' in Northern Uganda is hard for an Irish person to understand. Women are generally married off for their “Bride Price” which is a gift of livestock or money from the groom’s family to the brides in exchange for their daughter’s hand in marriage. As such, women are traditionally considered the property of men and it’s not uncommon that a man will beat their wife. It is often the girls' and women in the household whose duty it is to ensure the physical hard work is done in the household. When her husband dies, she will often have no have right to own land or to their hut as this would be passed to her late husband’s family, leaving her and her children homeless, scraping to get by and destined for a life of suffering.

What Rights?

Most women in Northern Uganda do not know their rights, for example, their right

  1. to decide over their own bodies, including contraception
  2. to health & education
  3. to say no to their husband
  4. to land

MS and ActionAid Uganda informing and educating women to know their rights and use them if they are violated.

We are pushing at the local and national authorities, to boys and girls and men and women are treated with respect and have equal access to, for example, public care, education and influence in society.

We promise tangible improvements in 2017 - women's rights and gender equality

  1. Fewer women and girls subjected to violence and abuse
  2. Girls and women have better access to rights for sexual and reproductive health
  3. Girls and women have improved their level of education. The 2013 figures: 65% early school leavers.
  4. More women are involved in decision-making and local committees.
  5. Women have higher income and control over household finances
  6. Women have greater access to and control over land.
  7. Increased hand lifting of laws and policies that promote women's and girls' rights.
